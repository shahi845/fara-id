How Islamic Inheritance Works

Islamic inheritance (Farā’iḍ) is a mathematically precise system that ensures wealth is distributed among surviving relatives according to divine injunctions. The Shāfiʿī methodology calculates shares using a strict order of operations:

1. Sharers (Aṣḥāb al-Furūḍ)

These are heirs with fixed fractional shares explicitly mentioned in the Quran (e.g., 1/2, 1/4, 1/8, 2/3, 1/3, 1/6). They are given priority. Examples include the spouse, parents, and certain siblings.

2. Residuaries (ʿAṣabah)

After the fixed Sharers take their portions, the remaining estate is given to the Residuaries. These are typically male relatives (sons, brothers, uncles). If males and females of the same degree inherit together as residuaries (e.g., sons and daughters), the male receives twice the share of the female.

3. Blocking (Ḥajb)

Closer relatives often exclude more distant ones. For example, a father completely blocks a grandfather from inheriting, and a son blocks all siblings. This is called total blocking (Ḥajb Ḥirmān).

4. ʿAwl (Proportional Reduction)

Sometimes the sum of all fixed fractions exceeds 1 (e.g., a husband, two sisters, and a mother). In this case, the base denominator is proportionally increased so all shares are reduced fairly.

5. Radd (Redistribution)

If there is surplus wealth after giving the Sharers their fixed portions and there are no Residuaries alive, the surplus is redistributed back to the Sharers (excluding spouses) in proportion to their original shares.

Fiqh Methodology

This calculator is based strictly on the Shāfiʿī School of Jurisprudence. It accurately implements:

  • Ḥajb (Exclusion / Blocking)
  • ʿAwl (Proportional Reduction)
  • Radd (Redistribution)
  • Gharāwiyyatayn / ʿUmariyyatayn (Special parent/spouse cases)
  • ʿAṣabah Rules (Including the 2:1 male-to-female ratio and complex extended relatives)
